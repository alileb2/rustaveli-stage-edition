# Scene III from "The Knight in the Panther's Skin"

## Georgian Text

მეფესა ესე ამბავი უჩს, ვითა მღერა ნარდისა,
უხარის ეგრე სიკეთე მისისა განაზარდისა,
აქვს მიჯნურობა ამისი, ვითა ბულბულსა ვარდისა,
სიცილით ლაღობს, მიეცა გულით,
და მოწამე მისი, მე ვინმე მოვიდე,
საუკუნოდ ვერ მოვკვდები, ვერ მოვკვდები.

(განაგრძობა...)

## English Translation

*(Translation by Marjory Wardrop, 1912)*

This tale of kings is like a melody of nard,
Such joy and goodness in its growth are stored,
It holds a bond as of the nightingale and rose,
With laughter it shines, given from the heart,
And its witness, I, someone who came,
Forever I shall not die, I shall not die.

(Continued...)

## Annotations

### On Individual Freedom

The lines "Forever I shall not die, I shall not die" reflect Rustaveli's philosophy of the immortal spirit of the individual who asserts their will and freedom. In the Georgian medieval context, this can be read as a defiance of feudal subjugation and an affirmation of personal agency.

### The Nightingale and Rose

The simile of the nightingale (ბულბული) and the rose (ვარდი) is a classical Persian poetic motif that Rustaveli adapts to express the interdependence of love and beauty. Here it underscores the theme of harmonious union, which can be staged as a visual metaphor of intertwined fates.

### Staging Suggestion

A modern production might render this scene through shadow play, with the nightingale and rose as silhouettes that gradually merge, while a chorus chants the Georgian text and a solo voice speaks the English translation. This juxtaposition highlights the dialogue between tradition and contemporary interpretation.
